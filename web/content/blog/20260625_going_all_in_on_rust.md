---
title: Going All-In on Rust
description: Why Neon Law Foundation chose one language for fast, safe, local-first access-to-justice software.
---

![Ferris, the Rust logo, and the Neon Law Foundation banner](img/going-all-in-on-rust/ferris-rust-logo-nlf.png)

We are going all-in on Rust.

The reason is simple: Rust can work almost anywhere. The same language can power a Windows laptop, a Linux server, a
cloud container, a local dev box, a command-line validator, a web server, a workflow worker, and whatever comes next.
For Neon Law Navigator, that matters more than fashion. One stack means fewer seams, fewer handoffs, and fewer ways for
small teams to lose the thread.

That is the access-to-justice bet. A legal workflow should be something a person can run, inspect, and improve without
needing five runtimes or a perfect Internet connection. Cancer, divorce, caregiving, debt, immigration, formation work:
people reach for legal tools when life is already heavy. The software should open quickly, explain itself, and keep
working close to the machine in front of them.

Rust helps us build that kind of commons. [Neon Law Foundation](/foundation/mission) can steward the project in public;
Neon Law can run it in production; other lawyers, legal-aid groups, students, and builders can fork it, rebrand it, and
make it their own. The code is Apache-2.0 or MIT because the point is not to trap the work inside one firm. The point is
to make the work usable beyond us.

That is what all-in means: not that Rust is magic, and not that every problem becomes easy. It means we are choosing a
portable, inspectable foundation for legal software that should outlive any one company. Neon Law Navigator has to be
bigger than Neon Law.
